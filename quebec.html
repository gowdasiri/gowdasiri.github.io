Montreal & Quebec City

Bonjour, mes amis. Quebec is an eastern province in Canada with two main cities, Montreal and Quebec City. With the charm of Europe and the familiarity of North America, the province offers the best of both worlds.

Background

A Little History

The origins of Quebec date back to 1534–35, when the French explorer Jacques Cartier landed in what is now Québec City and took possession of the land in the name of the king of France.

Permanent European settlement began in 1608, when Samuel de Champlain established a fort at Cape Diamond — the site of present-day Quebec City. "Kebec" is an Algonquin word meaning "where the river narrows." At the time of European exploration, the entire region was fully settled and controlled by various Aboriginal groups.

Throughout the rest of the 17th century, the French and English skirmished over control of Canada. By 1759, the English won a final battle on the Plains of Abraham and established themselves as the colonial rulers. Thousands of British Loyalists later fled the American Revolution, and power struggles between the two language groups continued through the 1800s — until Lower Canada joined the Canadian confederation as Québec in 1867.

This region boasts the largest French-speaking population outside France. Make sure to brush up on some French before you arrive — even a few words go a long way.

Planning

When to visit

I recommend the summer months — July in particular. That said, the province is a year-round destination with very different characters depending on the season.

Summer (May to August)

  • Festivals — Keep an eye out for the Just for Laughs Comedy Festival and the International Jazz Festival in July. The city comes alive.
  • Cirque du Soleil — One of the most beloved circus shows in the world, started by a troupe of Montreal street performers. You can catch it performing in Montreal during the summer months.

Winter (October – March)

  • Winter sports & Christmas festivities — Snow tubing, snowmobiling, skiing, dog-sledding, snowshoeing and more. The region knows how to do winter.
  • Ice Hotel — Open January through March, with a Great Hall, chapel, ice slide, exhibitions, and an Ice Bar. Truly one of a kind.
Accommodation

Where to stay

Old Town Montreal is a great area to base yourself — walkable to all the main attractions. While it can be pricier than other parts of the city, it's worth it for the late nights you get to spend wandering its streets. There are also a number of hostels and Airbnbs in the area with comparable deals.

The city is beautiful at night — Notre Dame even has a light and music show at 9pm. Stay close to the action.

Day by day

3-Day Itinerary

Day 01

Arrival — Old Montreal

Place d'Armes Basilique Notre Dame Rue Saint-Paul Place Jacques-Cartier Marché Bonsecours Cirque du Soleil

Begin your walk at Place d'Armes, a beautiful and historic city square anchored by the Basilique Notre Dame. Check out the stunning stained glass windows depicting the history of the city inside — $8 entry ticket. Don't miss the statues at the corners of the square, a sassy work of art symbolizing the complicated cultural history between English and French Canadians.

Take a stroll down Rue Saint-Paul and find your way to the bustling Place Jacques-Cartier, the main town square of Montreal. During summer you'll find an array of musicians, street performers, and artists. Spend time exploring alleyways with cute shops, restaurants, and brasseries all around.

Walk northeast to visit boutiques at the historic Marché Bonsecours, or simply admire the beautiful buildings as you pass by. Make a pit stop at Modavie for some French soup, or join a food tour — I recommend Local Food Tour Montreal (book early, it fills up fast). For delectable desserts and macaroons, head to Maison Christian Faure, a well-known patisserie down the street.

Le Vieux Port de Montréal in the Old Town is a great place to walk along the waterfront. You can opt for a Free Walking Tour or explore by bike — Montreal is a city made for cyclists. In the evening, grab a quick snack before heading to Cirque du Soleil, performed in a tent setup in Vieux Port.

Cirque du Soleil tip: Tickets start from 100 CAD. Book at the ticket counter on the day for 10% off — sometimes up to 25% on select days. The distance from stage to last seat is only 17 meters, so any seat works. Try for one towards the front rather than the periphery.

Day 02

Day Trip — Quebec City

Pallaird Café Château Frontenac Plains of Abraham Escalier Casse-Cou Rue du Petit Champlain Place Royale

Quebec City is everything the province represents — a European getaway with a very Parisian flair, hidden in Canada. Small, quaint, and charming, it's the oldest city in the region. Pack comfortable shoes: the city rests on a hill and you'll be walking all day.

I took the Viva Canada train both ways from Montreal, but I'd recommend taking the train one way and the bus the other — train timings aren't frequent, and the bus gives you more flexibility. Note: the journey isn't particularly scenic.

Start your day with coffee and croissants at Pallaird on Rue Saint-Jean. Head towards the city center and the Fairmont Le Château Frontenac — one of the first castle-style hotels of its kind, built in the 1890s by the Canadian Railway. It towers over the city from the top of the hill. En route you'll pass numerous art galleries, boutiques, cafes, and alleyways with artists selling their work.

Walk along the front of the hotel facing the St-Lawrence river and check out the Plains of Abraham, where the British fought the French in a three-month siege that culminated in a 40-minute battle giving Britain control of most of France's eastern North American possessions.

Work up an appetite and try poutine for lunch — french fries, cheese curd, and brown gravy, originated here in the late 1950s. Chic Shack, known for locally sourced ingredients, is a great spot.

Head down the Escalier Casse-Cou (Breakneck Steps) to the oldest shopping avenue in North America, Rue du Petit Champlain. This charming street was the highlight of my day trip. Buy maple syrup or maple caramel candy. Wander the alleyways down to the St-Lawrence river — I got lost in one and stumbled onto a street with colorful umbrellas hanging overhead.

Stroll further to Place Royale, the birthplace of Quebec, where Champlain fortified the city. Sit at Le Maison Smith for a coffee and explore murals depicting old Québécois life. If you have time, take the 15-minute ferry to Île d'Orléans for gorgeous views of Quebec City from the water.

Day 03

Montreal — Bagels, Mont Royal & Departure

St-Viateur Bagel Shop Mont Royal Gay Village Quartier des Spectacles

The Québécois take enormous pride in their oven-baked bagels — and rightly so. Denser than New York bagels, with a crisp baked exterior, they're in a league of their own. Try an everything bagel for breakfast at one of the OGs: St-Viateur Bagel Shop or Fairmount Bagel.

I'd recommend the St-Viateur Bagel Shop on Mont-Royal. Take the metro from Old Town and spend time exploring the main street of the city before heading up.

Check out Gay Village or swing by the McGill University area, then take a bus to Mount Royal for a spectacular view of the city on a clear day. Simply wandering around the Quartier des Spectacles gives you a great sense of daily life here.

Grab an early three-course dinner at a fancy restaurant like Damas, or a quick bite at Le Dépanneur Café, before heading home.

Looking back

Trip highlights

01

Cirque du Soleil. Spectacular from any seat — and you won't believe you got 10% off by buying at the door.

02

Rue du Petit Champlain. The most charming street in North America. The umbrella alleyway alone is worth the trip.

03

The bagels. I was skeptical. I was wrong. Denser, crispier, sweeter — in a league of their own.

04

Plains of Abraham. Standing where two empires collided in 40 minutes — history hits differently in person.

05

Notre Dame at night. The light and music show at 9pm is free, unexpected, and genuinely beautiful.

06

Montreal's food scene. From Michelin-starred seafood to market patisseries — the city punches far above its weight.
