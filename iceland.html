Winter Girls Trip
to Iceland

Reykjavik & South Coast

Iceland was created about 70 million years ago, by lava that rose to the ocean's surface and cooled. Popularly known as the land of fire and ice — only 20–25% of the island is inhabitable. A place unlike anywhere else on Earth.

Getting around

How to get around?

When you fly into Iceland, you land at Keflavik Airport in the western part of the island. Keflavik to Downtown Reykjavik (DTR) takes about an hour — here are your options:

  1. Taxi — Convenient and cost effective (fixed rate) for larger groups of 4–8. No need to book in advance — grab one at the airport or use the Hopp app. About ISK 4000 for 15–20 mins.
  2. FlyBus — Scheduled every 30–60 mins, city centre and back. Most cost effective for smaller groups of 1–4. Book tickets in the arrival hall or on the FlyBus app.
  3. Rental car — Iceland's roads are impeccably maintained, but winter driving means ice, blizzards, and near-zero visibility. We chose to skip it — but if you go for it, go prepared.
Accommodation

Where to stay?

Highly recommend staying in downtown Reykjavik on arrival — walkable distance from restaurants, bars, and tour pick-up points. Reykjavik was officially established in 1786 and is home to two-thirds of all Icelanders.

Pro tip: When booking accommodation, check the walking distance to Harpa Concert Hall on Maps. Anything under 15 minutes is centrally located. Some listings say "downtown" but are actually a 25–30 minute walk.

Planning

Hours of daylight

We visited at the end of February / beginning of March when days were getting noticeably longer. Sunrise on March 1st was around 8:30am and sunset around 6:40pm — about 10 hours of daylight. Really important to factor in before planning a trip to the land of midnight sun!

Preparation

Packing list

It is always better to be over-prepared than under. The golden rule: layers, waterproof everything, and hand warmers (yes, multiple).

  • Outer layer — Water-resistant and windproof outer pants + waterproof jacket
  • Thermals — Fast-drying thermals so you're not stuck waiting for them to dry overnight
  • Hiking boots — Ankle support, waterproof, and breathable. (I wore Merrel Moab 3 Mid WP)
  • Socks — Merino wool hiking socks; multiple pairs
  • Gloves — Waterproof and windproof with a pocket for hand warmers
  • Hand warmers — Non-negotiable for the aurora hunts
  • Fleece, sweaters, winter parka — Stack the layers
  • Bathing suit — For the geothermal pools
  • Power bank — Cold drains batteries fast
  • SPF & sunglasses — On rare sunny days, the sun sits low and is extremely bright
  • Adapter + SIM card — European Type F socket
  • Snacks — Nut butter packets, protein bars for long driving days
Conditions

Weather

Expect rain and wind in winter — but we were blessed with clear skies all week, and were repeatedly reminded how incredibly lucky we were. Even with sunshine, it was extremely cold — especially standing in open fields watching aurora borealis for hours.

Day by day

4-Day Itinerary

Day 01

The Golden Circle

Thingvellir National Park Geysir Geothermal Area Gullfoss Waterfall Seljalandsfoss

Thingvellir National Park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site — the location of Althingi, where Viking nobles founded Iceland's first Parliament in 930 AD. It also hosts a seismic rift valley with an ever-expanding gap between the Eurasian and North American tectonic plates — the only visible land border between two continents.

Next was the Geysir Geothermal Area, home to Strokkur — the most active geyser, firing boiling water up to 30 meters every few minutes — and the now-dormant Great Geysir, which gave all geysers their name.

Gullfoss ("Golden Falls") is a stunning two-stage waterfall that gets its name from the golden hue its water takes cascading over dark brown rock. On the drive south to Seljalandsfoss, we caught a clear view of Hekla Volcano. We stayed at Hotel Geirland that night.

Day 02

Glacier Lagoon & Ice Cave

Jökulsárlón Glacier Lagoon Diamond Beach Vatnajökull Glacier Natural Ice Cave

We passed moss-carpeted lava fields, volcanically scarred deserts, and rock formations standing as vivid reminders of eruptions past. Jökulsárlón Glacier Lagoon lies in the shadow of the largest glacier in Europe — famed for glittering icebergs floating across its icy-blue surface.

Many icebergs wash up on Diamond Beach, shining like beautiful diamonds against the black sands. We even saw seals here! At Skaftafell Nature Reserve, a guide took us on a hike to a natural ice cave with both blue and black ice — Iceland's ice caves form each winter as summer meltwaters refreeze into spectacular sapphire pockets.

Day 03

South Coast

Fjaðrárgljúfur Canyon Reynisfjara Black Sand Beach Skógafoss Waterfall Northern Lights Hunt

We started with a short detour to Fjaðrárgljúfur Canyon — a beautiful hike up the mountain with stunning canyon views. Then the iconic Reynisfjara Black Sand Beach — the stark contrast of white-crested Atlantic waves against jet-black sands is both stunning and intimidating. A danger signal indicates safety levels: green (rare), yellow (stay behind markers), red (evacuate). It was yellow when we visited.

Skógafoss cascades 60 meters wide. We climbed the path beside the falls — extremely windy, but worth it. Folklore says a Viking settler hid a treasure chest beneath the falls. The famous Eyjafjallajökull stands above it. That night we returned to Reykjavik and went aurora hunting — more on that below!

Day 04

Active Volcano & Northern Lights Tour

Volcano Hike Evacuation Alert Northern Lights Tour

While the main crater was dormant, we saw fresh lava surrounding the area with fumes rising from the surface — and molten lava up close. Then, right at the end of the hike, we received an evacuation warning predicting another eruption in the Grindavík area within 30 minutes. We evacuated. On the drive back, we noticed a large crack in a frozen lake caused by one of the 40+ earthquakes that day.

That evening we joined a Northern Lights tour — available only September through April — to escape light pollution. We were blessed with the most spectacular aurora borealis two nights in a row.

Aurora guide

Northern Lights Guide

Tips to hunt Aurora Borealis

01

Clear night skies

Aurora appears in the thermosphere — above clouds. If you can spot stars easily, you'll be able to see the lights. Check cloud cover here.

02

Check the forecast

The Aurora forecast shows probability every 30 minutes. 2–3 is a good probability — we saw the lights with that score. This site shows live activity — flat lines = no activity, haphazard lines = go!

03

Escape light pollution

Get as far from city lights as possible. The moon is also your enemy — check moonrise times. On a clear night with low city lights, you're usually in for a show.

04

Dress impossibly warm

Layers, double socks, warmest gloves, hot hands. You're in for a long wait in open cold fields. We couldn't feel our fingers after a while.

05

Manifest (but also: check the forecast)

Northern lights don't run on a schedule. You can have perfect conditions and see nothing — or low probability and witness the most spectacular show of your life.

Photography tips

01

Bring a tripod

Absolutely essential. Long exposure + any hand shake = blurry images.

02

Exposure time

Start at 5 seconds and adjust. Longer exposure captures more light from the moving particles.

03

Low aperture

Set aperture as low as possible (bigger opening). Smaller numbers = more light in.

04

ISO 800

Start at 800 and adjust as needed. Higher ISO = more light sensitivity, but can cause grain.

Eating & drinking

Food Guide

The food scene in Reykjavik is popping — Michelin starred restaurants, food halls, fine dining, cafes, bars. Traditional Icelandic food centers around fish and lamb, with smoked, salted, and fermented preparations.

What to eat

Seafood

Arctic char, cod, fish & chips, soups — we had it almost every day and it was absolutely delicious every single time.

Rye bread

Freshly baked, soft with a cake-like texture and slightly sweet. Worth going out of your way for — one friend made an hour-long trip for the geothermal underground-baked version.

Skyr

Icelandic cultured dairy product with the consistency of strained yogurt. Try the Ornu plain-flavored one.

Vegan options

Every restaurant has at least one vegan dish. Apparently this is the norm across most Scandinavian countries.

Where to eat

  • Skal! at Hlemmur Food Hall — Michelin starred, modern Icelandic food. The Icelandic scallops with red currant granita and the dessert "some kind of pie" were unforgettable.
  • Kaffi Loki — Opposite Hallgrimskirkja, homemade rye bread topped with fresh fish. Get the rye bread ice cream (it tastes like butterscotch!).
  • Bread & Co. — The best cinnamon roll you'll ever have.
  • Cafe Babalu — Quirky, central, great for brunch. We recommend the berry mix crepes.
  • Hraðlestin — North Indian food cooked to perfection. Reasonably priced for Iceland.

Coffee culture

Icelanders receive only four hours of sunlight on the darkest day of the year. To cope, they love their coffee — Reykjavik boasts a remarkable café culture, and time of day is irrelevant. Our guide Hilmar summed it up: to deal with the lack of sun, Icelanders rely on coffee, Vitamin D supplements, and Prozac.

Restaurant etiquette

Tap water is not only safe but tastes better than bottled. Tipping is not customary — but welcome for exceptional service. Dinner is typically 7–8pm; reservations recommended for fine dining.

Nightlife

We returned from a northern lights hunt at 1am to find the streets of Reykjavik more alive than ever. DTR has a reputation as one of the hottest nightlife spots in the world. Weekdays till 1am, weekends till 4:20am. Stop by Kaldi Bar for local beers.

Safety

Safety

Iceland consistently ranks #1 on the Global Peace Index with one of the world's lowest crime rates and no dangerous animal predators. As a girls' group visiting remote spots for aurora hunting, we never felt unsafe — but always remain cautious.

For safety tips, renting a Personal Location Beacon (PLB), weather and road conditions, and live weather warnings, visit www.safetravel.is. Check the weather regularly before any drive, hike, or sailing — it is famous for changing in a moment's notice.

Looking back

Highlights from our trip

01

The waterfalls. So many, each stunning in their own way.

02

Northern Lights. Nothing beats the aurora hunt. Night one: we thought we'd found a spot, didn't see it, got followed by a cat for an hour, then booked a cab to a remote golf course parking lot near a lighthouse. Saw it the moment we arrived. Froze for an hour and a half. Absolutely awestruck. Night two was a breeze by comparison. Two consecutive nights of the most magical thing I've ever seen.

03

Glacier Lagoon and Diamond Beach. Icebergs, seals, and melted ice crystals on black sand that look like museum glass. Beautiful and one of a kind.

04

Glacier and ice cave hike. Surreal blue and black ice you have to see to believe.

05

Ice water plunge and thermal bath. 25 geothermal pools in the Reykjavik area. I was surprised to discover I loved the cold plunge (for 20 seconds only). Love that this is how they socialize.

06

Volcano hike and evacuation alert. 40 earthquakes that morning, an evacuation warning predicting eruption within 30 minutes, and a freshly cracked frozen lake on the drive back. Iceland keeps you on your toes.

07

Reynisfjara Black Sand Beach. Watching waves rise tens of feet in seconds makes you understand why the warning signal is almost always yellow. Stunning and humbling.
