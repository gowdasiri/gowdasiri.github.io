Best time to visit

The best time to visit Tulum, Mexico, depends on what you prioritize for your trip, such as weather conditions, avoiding crowds, and budget considerations. Here's a breakdown of the different seasons:

High Season (December to April)

  • Weather: This period offers the best weather, with warm temperatures, low humidity, and minimal rainfall.
  • Crowds: It is the peak tourist season, particularly around Christmas, New Year, and Easter. Beaches and attractions will be busier.
  • Cost: Accommodation and flights are more expensive due to high demand.
  • Events: Various festivals and events take place, adding vibrancy to the experience.

Shoulder Seasons (May to June and November)

  • Weather: Still relatively warm and enjoyable, though there can be occasional rain showers.
  • Crowds: Fewer tourists compared to the high season, making it a great time to visit if you prefer a quieter experience.
  • Cost: More affordable than the high season, with lower rates for hotels and flights.
  • Activities: Beaches and cenotes are less crowded, making for a more peaceful visit.

Low Season (July to October)

  • Weather: This is the rainy season, with the highest chance of hurricanes from August to October. Humidity is high, and there can be heavy rainfall.
  • Crowds: The least crowded time, offering a more tranquil experience.
  • Cost: Significantly lower prices for accommodation and flights, ideal for budget travelers.
  • Activities: Some businesses may close or have limited hours, but the natural beauty and cultural sites are still accessible.

Best Overall Time to Visit

For the best balance of good weather, fewer crowds, and reasonable prices, November and early December are often recommended. These months fall just before the high season begins, offering pleasant weather and a quieter atmosphere.

Hurricanes are most likely between August and October. While this doesn't mean a hurricane will definitely occur, it's something to consider when planning your trip.

Itinerary

Day 1: Arrival and Beach Relaxation

  • Morning: Arrive in Tulum (Tulum airport is a 30-40 minute drive from downtown/beach area) and check into your hotel.
  • Afternoon: Head to Playa Paraiso, one of Tulum’s most stunning beaches. Relax, swim, and enjoy the Caribbean vibes.
  • Evening: Dine at a beachfront restaurant like La Eufemia Beach Club to enjoy delicious food with a view.

For a more chill atmosphere, you can bring your own towel and drinks and head to one of Tulum’s true public-access beaches, which are equally beautiful: 

  • Las Palmas
  • Playa Paraiso
  • Playa Ruinas 

Day 2: Tulum Ruins and Cenotes

  • Morning: Visit the Tulum Ruins early to beat the crowds and heat. Enjoy the scenic views of the ancient Mayan port city.
  • Afternoon: Cool off with a swim in Sac Actun, a half an hour drive from the ruins. You have to book a guided tour here, which involves a combination of walking and snorkeling (mostly snorkeling), but it really is worth it! I couldn't recommend it highly enough. The tour lasts about 90 mins and you get to marvel at the underground connected cave system. With the price of your tour, you'll also receive all the gear you need, including a wetsuit and snorkel equipment. 

Other cenote recommendations :

  • Oxman
  • Gran Cenote
  • Dos Ojos
  • Car Wash - crystal clear waters
  • Casa Cenote
  • Zacil-Ha
  • Calavera 
  • Casa Tortuga

Whichever cenotes you choose, plan to pay an entrance fee (minimum $8 USD) plus an additional fee for any camera or drones you plan on bringing in with you.

  • Evening: Explore Tulum Pueblo (town) and have dinner at Bonita's or Burrito Amor for tasty, casual dining.

Day 3 : Day Trip to Chichén Itzá, Valladoid and Cenote Ik Kil

  • Morning: Depart EARLY (cannot stress this enough !! Sharp 7 at the latest) for Chichén Itzá, one of the New Seven Wonders of the World. It's a 2-2.5 hour drive from Tulum. Explore the main pyramid, El Castillo, the Temple of the Warriors, and the Great Ball Court. You'll find tour guides close to the gate and even at the gate. Make sure they speak your language of choice for the tour and fluently, before hiring them.
  • Afternoon: Cool off at a cenote! There are some pretty epic cenotes near Chichen-Itza. Try to visit at least one!  Ik Kil (less than a 10 minute drive from the Chichen Itza and absolutely stunning!), Lorenzo Oxman and Suytun to name a few.
  • Lunch : Haciendo Chichen is a gorgeous Spanish mansion converted to a hotel and is a great spot for lunch, located a few mins away from the Chichen Itza.
  • Late Afternoon: Next to Quintana Roo, the state of Yucatán is home to a slew of charming, colorful, colonial towns, with Valladolid being one of the closest. While many skip over Valladolid to head straight back to Tulum, this city is well worth a stop in its own right. Spend some time shopping, visiting the church and getting some churros in the park.
  • Evening: Return to Tulum and relax. Have dinner at a local restaurant like Palma Central.

Day 4 : Sian Ka’an Biosphere Reserve

  • Morning: Take a guided tour of the Sian Ka’an Biosphere Reserve. This UNESCO World Heritage Site offers opportunities to see diverse wildlife, mangroves, and possibly dolphins, turtles and manatees.
  • Afternoon: Continue the tour with activities like bird watching and boat rides through the lagoons. The tour takes you near the world's second largest coral reef where you can snorkel. Stop for lunch in Punta Allen, a small village on the Eastern tip of Mexico, where you get to savor some fresh delicious seafood.
  • Evening: Head back to Tulum and dine at Mateo's, known for its fish tacos.

Day 5: Day Trip to Playa del Carmen

  • Morning: Get breakfast in Tulum and head to Cenote Azul. Cenote Azul is a beautiful cenote with different shades of blue, located near Playa del Carmen, a 20-minute drive away. It’s possible to swim and snorkel in its refreshing water. Cenote Chaak Aktun is another option, with a cave tour. More Cave tours
  • Afternoon: Akumal beach - try your luck swimming with sea-turtles. Rent life vests and head to the beach. Stay near areas with seaweed as that's the main reason the sea turtles are found near the beach (seagrass and nesting).
  • Late Afternoon : Drive to Playa del Carmen, about an hour from Tulum. Explore Fifth Avenue (La Quinta Avenida), a pedestrian street filled with shops, restaurants, and cafes. Get lunch in Playa del carmen. Eat at a restaurant with a 2nd floor view of La Quinta - people watching is a blast.
  • Evening : Head back to Tulum to check in to your hotel and dinner at Mezzanine.

Playa del Carmen recommendations by https://sarowly.me/blog/playadelcarmen

Day 6: Relaxation and Departure

  • Morning: Spend your last morning at Mezzanine - enjoy the facilities, the beach, and a leisurely breakfast or brunch.
  • Afternoon: Depart for the airport.

Tips

  • Transportation: Renting a car in Mexico or using a mix of public transportation and taxis is convenient for getting around Tulum and for day trips. Read the road rules of Mexico and also familiarize yourself with some basic Spanish to read road signs.

$200 USD for a SUV rental from Hertz at Tulum airport (with full coverage) for 6 days. We had to wait about an hour to pickup our car. Drop off was slightly easier, but still took about half an hour. Factor in another $50 -$70 USD for diesel. Worth noting that some gas stations only take cash, and there are people who help fill the gas at the stations and wipe your glass clean.

  • Cash: Quite a few places prefer cash, tipping is also easier in cash, so have some pesos on hand.
  • Travel Insurance: We used IMG and Berkshire Hathway about $25 and $7 each for a 6-day trip. WorldNomads was another recommended insurance for adventurous travellers. https://upgradedpoints.com/travel/best-travel-insurance-mexico/
  • Reservations: Popular restaurants and tours often require reservations, so book in advance.

How is a cenote formed?

Cenotes are formed through a geological process involving the dissolution of limestone bedrock over thousands of years. Here’s a step-by-step explanation of how cenotes are formed:

  1. Limestone Formation: Cenotes typically form in regions where the bedrock is composed of limestone. Limestone is a porous rock formed from the accumulation of marine organisms such as corals and shellfish over millions of years.
  2. Underground Rivers and Caves: Rainwater picks up carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and soil, creating a weak carbonic acid solution. As this mildly acidic water percolates through cracks and faults in the limestone, it gradually dissolves the rock, enlarging the fractures and forming underground channels and caves.
  3. Roof Collapse: Over time, the ceilings of these underground caves and channels may become unstable due to the erosion of the limestone around them. Eventually, the ceilings collapse, exposing the underground water to the surface and forming a cenote.
  4. Cenote Characteristics: The resulting cenote can vary in size and shape, from small openings to large sinkholes. They often have steep, vertical walls and can be filled with freshwater, saltwater, or a mix of both, depending on their location and connection to underground aquifers.
  5. Hydrogeological Importance: Cenotes are essential as sources of freshwater in regions like the Yucatán Peninsula, where surface water is scarce. They also serve as windows into the underground water systems and are crucial for understanding local hydrogeology and biodiversity.

In summary, cenotes are formed through the dissolution of limestone by acidic groundwater, leading to the collapse of cave ceilings and the exposure of underground water systems to the surface. This geological process occurs gradually over long periods, shaping the unique landscapes found in cenote-rich regions like Mexico and Central America.

Beach Club

COCO TULUM

If you are visiting Tulum on a budget, Coco Beach Club is a good choice since their minimum consumption is only $25. While that sounds like a lot for a lunch, prices all along the beach are usually a lot higher. 

Their menu offers typical Tulum beach fare but the quality is amazing. Three fish tacos provide a generous portion and come with yummy spicy pineapple salsa, tomato salsa, and guacamole. Sandwiches (how about deep-fried prawns?!) come with a side of potatoes and they definitely don’t skimp on the good stuff. 

PAPAYA PLAYA PROJECT

The Papaya Playa Project, short PPP, is probably one of the hippest hotels and beach clubs in Tulum and you will have to pay for the honor to get in. During low season you can usually get in for a minimum consumption of $25 - $65 USD and chances are you will spend it rather quickly.

They offer both a beach club menu and a restaurant menu for lunch and dinner. At the beach club, you can get various ceviches, tacos and tostadas as well as some salads. I over-ordered as I wasn’t sure of the portion sizes – chances are you will be absolutely fine getting just one main dish as the portions are huge and come with a generous side of chips. 

If you are looking for something fresh, try the fish ceviche with onions and avocado, or for something a bit more indulgent the tuna tostada. You will need a fork as they definitely don’t skimp on the tuna and the tostadas are loaded with avocado mousse. 

Some other beach clubs on a budget :

  • Taquería la Eufemia - No minimum and no cover charge
  • Paraiso Beach Club - $5 USD minimum spend
