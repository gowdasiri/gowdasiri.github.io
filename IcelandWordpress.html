Iceland was created about 70 M years ago, by lava that rose to the ocean's surface and cooled. Iceland's harsh environment - due to its proximity to the Arctic Circle - is tempered by the Gulf Stream bringing heat from the tropics. It was discovered by Norse people in the Viking Age. Popularly known as the land of fire and ice - due to its volcanic and icy terrains - only 20-25% of the island is inhabitable, predominantly on the eastern and southern coasts.

How to get around?

When you fly into Iceland, you fly into Keflavik airport, located in the Western part of the island. Keflavik to Downtown Reykjavik (DTR) takes about an hour, and there are many different ways to get there.

  1. Take a taxi - Convenient and cost effective (fixed rate) for larger groups of 4-8. There’s no need to book one in advance, you can get one at the airport or book one on the Hopp app. Rides cost about ISK 4000 for 15-20 mins of ride time.
  2. Take a FlyBus - They're scheduled every half hour or hourly, from airport to the city centre and vice versa. This is the most cost effective option for a smaller group size of 1-4 and you can get tickets in the arrival hall or book them on the FlyBus app.
  3. Rent a car - Given that the weather is unpredictable and changes in a moment's notice especially in the winter in Iceland, we heavy heartedly chose to forgo this option (didn't want to risk getting caught in a blizzard or snowstorm). If this doesn't deter you, it's worth noting here that Iceland's roads are impeccably well maintained. It's hard to believe that a country with long winters has roads this great, but it does! Be prepared though, as they get icy and visibility is minimal (about 20 feet) when it's snowing or raining.

Where to stay?

I would highly recommend staying in downtown Reyjavik on arrival, as it's a walkable distance from restaurants, bars, pick up points, etc. Reykjavik was officially established in 1786 and is home to two third of Icelanders, with a population of about 135000.

Most of downtown is walkable and pick up points for tour buses and public transportation are a short walk as well(ours was only a couple of minutes away). If you're booking an Airbnb like we did, sometimes the accommodation is listed in DTR, even though in reality it's a 25-30 mins walk from the city. When you find an accommodation, put the address on maps, and check the walking distance to Harpa Concert hall, any place that's under a 15 mins walk is centrally located.

Hours of daylight

We planned our trip was towards the end of February and beginning of March, when the days were getting noticeably longer (thank god!). Sunrise on the 1st of March was at around 8:30 am and sunset was around 6:40 pm, which gave us around 10 hours of daylight! 

Really important to check before planning a trip to the land of midnight sun!

Weather

In general, expect a lot of rain and wind in the winter, and it can get very very cold. Surprisingly, we were blessed with clear skies and sunlight the whole week of the trip, and were repeatedly reminded of how incredibly lucky we were. Even with sunshine all day long, it was extremely cold. This was especially true on the nights we watched aurora borealis for hours in open areas! So pack lots of warm layers, hot hands (yes, you'll need them!) and waterproof windproof clothing. My takeaway from this trip in terms of packing was that it’s always better to be over prepared than under.

Itinerary

Day 1 Golden Circle

  • Thingvellir National Park
  • Geysir Geothermal area
  • Gullfoss waterfalls
  • Seljalandsfoss waterfalls

The first day of our adventure began with the Golden Circle route – a trio of legendary attractions in Iceland.

Thingvellir National Park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site due to its geological and historical importance. It’s the original location of Althingi – a dramatic glacial plain where a group of Viking nobles founded the first Icelandic Parliament in 930 AD. Thingvellir also hosts a seismic rift valley with an ever-expanding gap between the Eurasian and North American tectonic plates. A unique place that is the only land visible land border between two continents.

Our next stop was the Geysir Geothermal area, where we got to see the now dormant Great Geysir – which was the first geyser to appear in the English language and gives them all their name. Alongside Strokkur – the most active geyser in the area – firing boiling torrents of water up to 30 meters into the air every few minutes or so.

The final stop on the Golden Circle route was Gullfoss (translates to “the Golden Falls”) – a stunningly beautiful two-stage waterfall that gets its name from the golden color its water takes as it cascades over the dark brown rock.

After visiting the Golden Circle, we drove southwards to Seljalandsfoss waterfalls, which was magnificent! Since it was a clear day, we even got a view of Hekla Volcano on the way!

We then headed to Hotel Geirland hotel for the night.

Day 2 Jokulsarlon Glacier Lagoon and ice cave

  • Jokulsarlon Glacier Lagoon
  • Diamond Beach
  • Vatnajokull Glacier and a natural ice cave hike

We headed to Jokulsarlon Glacier Lagoon and the nearby Diamond Beach – passing moss-carpeted lava fields, volcanically scarred deserts, and some spectacular rock formations that stand as vivid reminders of volcanic eruptions over the years. We also caught glimpse of the Breidamerkurjokull Glacier on the way, which unfurled like a tongue from Vatnajokull, the largest glacier in Europe.

The Jokulsarlon Glacier Lagoon lies in its shadow, famed for the glittering icebergs that float across its icy-blue surface. Many of them wash up on the nearby Diamond Beach, where they shine like beautiful diamonds against the black sands. We even saw seals here!

After Jokulsarlon, we headed to Skaftafell Nature Reserve where a guide took us on a short hike to the mesmerizing Vatnajokull Glacier and a natural ice cave. Iceland's ice caves usually form over the winter months. When the meltwaters from summer freeze, they create huge pockets and beautiful patterns within the sapphire-blue ice. The cave we visited had both blue and black ice.

We stayed at Adventure Hotel Geirland this night too.

Day 3 - South Coast

  • Fjaðrárgljúfur Canyon
  • Vik
  • Reynisfjara Black Sand Beach
  • Skogafoss Waterfall

We started our tour along the South Coast, making a small detour to Fjaðrárgljúfur Canyon, which was beautiful and the short hike up the mountain with stunning views of the canyon was fun.

Next, we visited the Eldhraun lava field before heading to the iconic Reynisfjara Black Sand Beach. The stark contrast of the white-crested waves of the Atlantic Ocean as they wash against its jet-black sands is stunning and scary at once. There is a danger signal to indicate how safe it is to go close to the water, green means safe (apparently it's hardly ever green), yellow mean stay behind the markers and (it was yellow when we where) and red means get out of the beach! From the cliffside rises a cluster of basalt sea stacks, reminiscent of the Giant’s Causeway in Ireland. We also saw the Reynisdrangar basalt pillars, which rise like jagged peaks from the sea. Folklore has it that there were once a trio of trolls turned to stone by the sun as they tried to drag a boat back to land.

Skogafoss Waterfall is spectacular, and has a wider cascade that tumbles down 60 meters into a pool below. We climbed up the meandering path next to the falls – for a workout than for the view given it was extremely windy to enjoy the spectacular vista over the entire area. Some locals also believe that a Viking settler hid a treasure chest somewhere beneath the falls – offering fabulous wealth to the person who finds it.

The famous Eyjafjallajökull stands above the Skogafoss waterfall, and with good visibility, you can catch a glimpse of this magnificent mountain.

We returned to Reykjavik for the night and went aurora hunting and saw the most stunning lights, but more on that in the section dedicated for the northern lights.

Day 4 - Active Volcano and Northern Lights tour

  • Volcano hike
  • Northern lights tour

Eruptions are rare events, and not all eruptions are created the same, some spew ashes far up into the sky while others pump out molten magma.

While we didn't see boiling lava, since the main crater is dormant, we were able to see the fresh lava surrounding the area, with fumes coming out of the surface. We were also able to see molten lava up close, which was pretty amazing.

And true to the Icelandic experience, we received an evacuation warning right at the end of the hike, predicting another eruption, as soon as 30 mins, in the recently erupted Grindvik area and were asked to evacuate right away.

On return to Reykjavik, we grabbed dinner at Skal! and went to the pick up stop for the Northern lights tour. While we had seen the lights the prior night too, we wanted to rely on professionals to take us far away from the city lights to escape light pollution, to see the brightest lights yet. Nothern Lights tour is available only during the winter season from September to April.

Northern Lights Guide

Tips to hunt Aurora Borealis

  1. Clear night skies - Since the lights appear in the thermosphere, above the clouds, they can't be seen on cloudy nights (and many winter nights are cloudy). And a good indicator to check is if you're able to spot the stars easily, which means you will be able to see the aurora, in case of activity. Use this website to check for cloud cover.
  2. Check the forecast - Aurora borealis forecast website shows you the probability of seeing the lights on any given night. 5 indicates maximum probability and it is never 5, and 0 means highly improbable. Generally 2-3 are good probabilities, and we saw the lights with that prediction. The forecast is for every half hour, so check frequently during the hours when the sky is clear. This website shows the activity, when the lines are flat it indicates minimal or no activity and when they are haphazard, indicating activity, that's the time to head out for the hunt!
  3. Away from the lights - Aurora borealis is also not very bright, so wait until it's dark to go out and hunt. While the winters have long nights, with only four hours of sunlight in peak winter, summer is the opposite with where the sun hardly ever sets. It never gets dark enough to spot the lights in the summer. For these reasons, it's important to find a place away from city lights, like remote places outside of the city areas to view auroras. In the case you can't, try to have the lights behind you, and on a good night you'll be able see it, albeit faintly due to the light pollution. The moon is also your enemy because on a clear night it lights up the sky like a torch, so check the moon rising as well.
  4. Dress warm - Wear layers, double sock up, wear your warmest gloves and make sure to carry hot hands. It gets cold staying out in the night and the winds can make the night even colder. You're also in for a wait, since the aurora is really more of a show, so even if you spot it right away, you would want to stay and watch them change form. We really couldn't feel our fingers and toes after a while.
  5. Manifest (just kidding, but not entirely kidding!) - The northern lights are a natural phenomenon and they don't appear on a schedule. You can see a good forecast and not see the aurora, or on a night with low probability, see the most spectacular show. Worth noting here, that we heard that on most nights with clear skies, you're in for a show!

Basic Photography tips to capture the lights

  1. Tripod - This is an absolute necessity for clear images, something we didn't really quite think about before heading for the hunt. Since we're trying to capture moving particles in the night, to capture more of the lights we need to increase the exposure time. However, that means any shake to the phone causes the image to blur.
  2. Camera settings - As mentioned above, since we're trying to catch moving particles we need to increase the exposure time - start with 5s and adjust as needed.
  3. Set the aperture low. Aperture dictates how much light your lens lets in. You want it to be as open as possible to capture as much of the lights as you can. Smaller numbers mean bigger opening.
  4. ISO dictates how sensitive the camera is to the light. Usually when set too high it can cause the images to look grainy, but on a dark night it's essential - set to 800 and adjust as needed.

Food Guide

The food scene in all of Iceland, but especially Reykjavik is popping - with Michelin starred restaurants, food halls, fine dining, cafes and bars!

What to eat?

Traditional Icelandic food is usually centered around fish or lamb. Many traditional dishes are made with smoked, salted or fermented ingredients, preservation techniques used in times before refrigeration. Most of these dishes like, fermented shark, fish jerky, smoked lab, picked lamb testicles, etc. are an acquired taste. Other classics include fish & potato casserole, fish balls and broth based soup. An iconic dessert is Icelandic pancakes - crepes served with jam and cream. Don't be surprised to see delicacies like puffin, shark, whale horse or even reindeer on the menu.

  • Seafood was absolutely delicious and we had it almost everyday here! Seafood like arctic char, cod, and popular dishes like fish and chips and soups, can be found everywhere.
  • Rye bread Freshly baked rye is soft with a cake like texture and slightly sweet too. Extremely popular here and really worth trying.
  • Skyr Icelandic cultured daily product with the consistency of strained yogurt. Recommend the Ornu plain flavored one.
  • Vegan restaurants are found everywhere here and every restaurant has at least one vegan dish. Apparently, this is the norm in most Scandinavian countries.

Our trip ended with my gluten free friend wanting to make an hour long trip to get some geothermal underground rye(not to mention eating rye everyday of the trip) and my vegetarian friend having seafood almost everyday and leaving craving more. So it's safe to say, food here is outstanding!

Love for coffee

Located just south of the Arctic Circle means that on the darkest day of the year Iceland receives only four hours of sunlight. To cope with this, Icelanders love their coffee and Reykjavik boasts of a remarkable cafe culture - you'll find this in the numerous cafes all around the city. Time of day is unimportant - order coffee any time during the day or night. Hilmar (our affable guide) told us, that to cope with the lack of sun, Icelanders love their coffee, Vitamin D supplements and Prozac.

Restaurant etiquette

Icelandic tap water is not only safe, but tastes better than bottled water.

Tipping is not customary in Iceland and there is no service fee, but if you do want to tip exceptional service no one will be offended. Reservations are recommended, especially for fine dining restaurants and dinnertime is around 7pm-8pm.

Bar culture

While we weren't drinking on this trip, we returned from a northern lights hunt at 1am and found the streets of Reykjavik more alive than ever. DTR apparently has a reputation of having one of the hottest nightlife in the world. So after a day of exploring and hiking, trade in your hiking boots for party shoes, and see if you have enough stamina to stay up as long as a local. On weekdays most places are open till 1AM and on weekends till 4:20AM.

Stop by Kaldi Bar for a taste of Kale beer, or any of the city's beer bars and taste a variety of beverages produced in the country.

Safety

Iceland is a country with one of the world’s lowest crime rates, ranking consecutively in 1st place on the Global Peace Index, and home to no dangerous animal predators. Given that it was a girls trips and we went to many remote spots to hunt the northern lights, we never felt unsafe anywhere. However, always remain cautious.

For general safety tips when traveling through Iceland, renting a Personal Location Beacon (PLB), further weather and road conditions, and even live weather warnings on your phone, go to www.safetravel.is. Check the weather regularly, especially before heading to on drives, hikes or sailing, since the weather is famous for changing in a moment's notice.
