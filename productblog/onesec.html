One
Sec

App review
Friction · Mindfulness · Habit

In a day and age when everyone with access to the internet is operating on autopilot to some degree, Frederik Riedel's app reminds you to take a deep breath, pause, and rethink your intention before opening an app.

This simple yet ingenious idea reduces the amount of time you spend on social media by allowing you to mindfully use apps and enforcing scrolling with a purpose. Rather than removing friction to drive engagement — as virtually every other app does — One Sec adds it deliberately, at the moment that matters most.

"Many studies and books on behavior reiterate the role friction plays in both adoption and continued engagement. By introducing friction, One Sec reinforces conscious usage that most other apps simplify away."

The Theory

Habit loops & the role of friction

"Once our habits become automatic, we stop paying attention to what we are doing. The process of behavior change always starts with awareness. You need to be aware of your habits before you can change them." (p. 67)

"The Four Laws of Behavior Change: (1) make it obvious (2) make it attractive (3) make it easy (4) make it satisfying." (p. 55)

"To build a habit you need to practice it. And the most effective way to make the practice happen is to adhere to the 3rd Law: make it easy." (p. 147)

— Atomic Habits, James Clear

"First, understand the reason people use a product or service. Next, lay out the steps the customer must take to get the job done. Finally, once the series of tasks from intention to outcome is understood, simply start removing the steps until you reach the simplest possible process. Easier equals better."

— Hooked, Nir Eyal (summarising Denis J. Hauptly)

One Sec does exactly the opposite of that. A simple solution to a rather complicated problem. By adding friction, not only does it reduce the craving to open an app every ten seconds — it introduces mindfulness and allows you to work the app, rather than letting the prediction algorithm work you.

How it works

Features

Setup is straightforward. One Sec has a single core feature: it triggers when you open the host app. The screen asks you to take a deep breath as it introduces a pause — a moment of intentional interruption between the unconscious tap and the scroll.

The friction is the feature. There's nothing else to configure, no content to consume, no engagement loop. Just a breath and a question: do you actually want to open this right now?

Measuring success

Metrics

Success for One Sec is directly linked to usage of the app it monitors — a north star that sits in tension with most product metrics, which optimize for more engagement, not less.

01
Reduced time spent on the host app is the north star metric. Less time on Instagram after using One Sec means it's working.
02
Reduced number of app opens indicates that the unconscious trigger is becoming more conscious — you're aware of when and why you're opening it.
03
User-reported intentionality — gathering qualitative feedback on whether users feel the pause enables more purposeful usage. Harder to measure, but arguably the most meaningful signal.
Thinking further

A suggested improvement

Currently, One Sec triggers at the moment you open an app. A natural extension would be to trigger it after a fixed duration of use — say, every five minutes of Instagram — forcing a pause mid-session as well as at the entry point.

Since the app already makes you aware of how many times you've opened it and your total time spent, this would address a related problem: most of us use apps far longer than we intended without realising it. A mid-session pause could be a meaningful barrier to that drift.

"One Sec is a product built entirely against the grain of how apps are designed. It deliberately makes itself harder to use — and that's the point. It's rare to see a product that treats your attention as something worth protecting rather than something to be captured."
