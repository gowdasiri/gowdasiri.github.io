Threads

Product breakdown
Social · Algorithm · Broadcasting

Threads — undeniably a Twitter clone, advertised as a safer, cleaner alternative — had an incredibly high conversion rate. It launched into a market of disaffected Twitter users and Instagram's existing billion-person network simultaneously.

So far, I'm enjoying it. But it definitely feels like a stripped-down version of Twitter. No hashtags, heavy on the influencers — and the worst part is, a lot of the people in my replies don't seem to get the jokes that usually do well on Twitter. Threads is a Twitter clone, but Meta is introducing the concept to never-tweeters who have been on Instagram. So there's going to be an awkward phase of acclimating.

Comparison

Threads vs. Twitter

Threads

Algorithmic feed — interest-based, like Facebook or Instagram. Posts can be 5 hours or 5 minutes old.
500 character limit
No hashtags
Heavy on influencers and branded content
Draws from Instagram's existing social graph

Twitter

Reverse chronological timeline — every post from people you follow, in order.
280 character limit (free accounts)
Hashtags and trending topics
Indispensable for breaking news and live events
237.8 million daily active users (July 2022)

Meta's interest-based algorithm is a major turnoff for Twitter natives. It fills the feed with posts from accounts you don't follow — primarily influencers and brands — rather than the people you actually chose to connect with.

The Bigger Picture

Broadcasting platforms, not social networks

The truth is Twitter isn't a social network — and neither is Threads. Both are broadcasting platforms for big brands, celebrities, politicians, and media outlets to share information with followers. This type of network isn't conducive to how people actually socialize in communities.

"In social clubs, people congregate in smaller groups around shared interests. They don't crowd into an enormous conference room and shout at each other."

Meta knows this. Zuckerberg has said that people were increasingly shifting away from large public platforms toward smaller, more siloed networks — private Facebook groups and messaging apps. There's something fun about the performative element of talking in the public sphere, where conversations take on a different tone onstage in front of an audience. But it often gets very unfun very quickly.

On algorithmic bias, Jack Dorsey framed it well:

"Bias in algorithms is an important topic. Our responsibility is to understand, measure, and reduce accidental bias due to factors such as the quality of the data used to train our algorithms. This is an extremely complex challenge facing everyone applying artificial intelligence."

— Jack Dorsey, Sep 5, 2018
