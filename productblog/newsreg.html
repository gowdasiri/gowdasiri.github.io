News Publishers
& Tech Platforms

Power, money & regulation
Google · Meta · Journalism

The relationship between news publishers and technology companies — perceived to be symbiotic in the early days of the internet — has shifted to one of extreme imbalance, with platforms holding the majority of the power.

When the internet boom began, free news content gave technology companies like Google credible information to scrape, helping attract users and improve recommendations — for both content and advertising. In return, larger news organizations gained additional traffic through platform links, while lesser-known publishers gained discovery they couldn't have achieved alone.

This wasn't always the case. As more users came to rely on online platforms to stay informed and entertained, and as recommendation algorithms grew smarter with more data, the dynamic shifted drastically.

The Shift

How the balance tipped

01
Technology companies scrape news publishers' content for free, providing valuable results to users without them having to click through to the news site at all.
02
Recommendation algorithms grew smarter with more user data over time. A user is far more likely to click a curated vacation ad based on their prior search patterns than an objective ad on a news site — generating more ad revenue for the platform, even when the connection was originally made through a news article.
03
Tech company algorithms have the power to promote or demote a news publisher's content with minute tweaks — creating a non-objective, unreliable dependency that no publisher, however large, can safely challenge.

Platforms went even further: content on news sites began to be scraped to present answers directly in snippets — Google Search being the clearest example — allowing users to have questions answered without ever leaving the platform. Platforms continued to grow their revenue while news outlets felt the sting of severely decreased advertising revenue and page views.

The Argument

Platforms as neutral medium

The long-held argument — and one tech companies still make — is that platforms like YouTube, Google Search, Facebook, and Instagram simply host content. They are a medium through which anyone wanting to share content to a wider audience can do so for free. Like a queue in a publisher-subscriber model. The platforms do not request that users post content. They are simply broadcasters that freely share what is made available to them.

This model worked well initially. It enabled the world to gain access to information for free. Google's stated mission — "to organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful" — seemed like a genuinely noble pursuit in that context.

"If all the content was subjective, these tech organizations would not have been able to become one-stop information shops. The news content lends invaluable credibility to their results — for no cost."

Today, news publishers sign up to create content at an increasingly low benefit-cost ratio. The imbalance is so high that no publisher, however large, could demand fair compensation without risk of being banned or demoted to the depths of search results. And the world needs both large and small news publishers to stay in business — not only for the major stories, but for local coverage, minority voices, and diverse perspectives that only smaller publishers can provide.

Regulation

Enter regulations

The only real path forward — to undo damage and move to an era where publishers are compensated for their data being used — is through regulation. This is not simple. Platforms have consistently responded to proposed legislation with the threat of removing news content entirely.

Australia

Passed — 2021

The first country to enact such legislation. Meta temporarily blocked news sharing in Australia before coming to a deal and lifting the ban. Google announced a three-year global agreement with News Corp to pay for news content.

Canada

Passed — 2023

The Online News Act designates platforms as "digital news intermediaries" (DNIs) and requires payment agreements with eligible news outlets. If no agreement is reached, an arbitration panel decides. Both Google and Meta responded by banning all Canadian news content on their platforms.

California

Shelved — 2023

The California Journalism Preservation Act would require tech firms to share advertising revenue derived from news articles with publishers. Meta stated it would be "forced" to remove news from Facebook and Instagram if the bill became law. Shelved for a year.

European Union

Enforcement ongoing

A copyright directive adopted in 2019 requires Google, Facebook, and other platforms to compensate news organizations for their content. Countries within the EU have been working to enforce it with varying degrees of success.

Additionally, the US Justice Department and eight states sued Google in January 2023, accusing the company of illegally abusing its monopoly over the technology that powers online advertising — the first antitrust lawsuit against a tech giant under President Biden.

Opinions

What the platforms say

Meta

The relationship between platforms and news publishers is being misrepresented — Meta is projected to benefit unevenly.
Posts with links to news articles make up less than 3% of what people see in their Facebook Feed. Meta doesn't solicit, need, or collect content from news websites. Users and publishers choose to share it themselves. Globally, more than 90% of organic views on article links are on links posted by the publishers themselves.
The Act would subsidize big broadcasters at the expense of independent publishers, skewing the playing field against smaller players.
"It makes no more sense to claim social media companies are taking money from publishers than to say car companies stole from the horse and cart industry."

This is a weak analogy. Car companies didn't steal from the horse and cart industry because cars provided a genuinely superior alternative. But platforms don't have a journalism wing — they rely on news publishers to create the content that keeps users on their platforms, generating ad revenue from a creation they didn't pay to build, either directly (training algorithms) or indirectly (increasing time-on-platform).

Google

Canada's Online News Act is broader than those in Australia and Europe — it puts a price on news story links in search results and can apply to outlets that don't produce news.
Google proposed the bill be revised to make the display of news content — rather than links — the basis for payment, and to specify that only businesses that produce news and adhere to journalistic standards are eligible.
