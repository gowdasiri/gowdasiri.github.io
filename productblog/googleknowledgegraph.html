Google's mission statement, as stated by it's founders Larry and Sergey, is to organize the world’s information and make it universally accessible and useful.

Knowledge panel

Introduced in 2012, knowledge panels are information boxes that appear on Google when you search for entities (people, places, organizations, things) that are in the Knowledge Graph. They are meant to help you get a quick snapshot of information on a topic based on Google’s understanding of available content on the web.

Knowledge panels are automatically generated, and information that appears in a knowledge panel comes from various sources across the web. In some cases, they work with data partners who provide authoritative data on specific topics like movies or music, and combine that data with information from other open web sources.

Entities whose information is included in knowledge panels (like prominent individuals or the creators of a television show) who are self-authoritative, Google provides ways for these entities to provide direct feedback. Therefore, some of the information displayed may also come from verified entities who have suggested edits to facts on their own knowledge panels.

Images that appear in the knowledge panel can come from several sources. One source is those individuals that have claimed their knowledge panels and selected a featured image from images available on the web. Other images (especially when there is a collection of multiple images) are a preview of Google Images results for the entity and are automatically sourced from across the web.

Knowledge panels are updated automatically as information changes on the web, but Google also considers changes in two main ways: directly from the entities depicted in the knowledge panel, and from general user feedback.1

By Google's own account

Their aim on the launch of the Knowledge panel has been to help users find the right thing, provide the best summary and enable users to go broader and deeper.

It does this by :

  • Decoding the ambiguity of language
  • To identify the facts most likely to be needed for each item, they go back to their users and study in aggregate what they’ve been asking Google about each item.
  • It’s not just a catalog of objects; it also models all these inter-relationships. It’s the intelligence between these different entities that’s the key.2

Highlight : “People also search for” feature

This particular feature is something that only Google is equipped to provide. While I was growing up, ChildKraft books were my absolute favorite, followed by Brittanica. There were endless stories and knowledge to acquire and you never ran out of new things that interested you. 'People also search for' presents all this even more seamlessly, with prompts to meander without you having to go looking for the related topic.

The con as always, remains to be the bias of it all. Assume that while most people find the actors drawing their attention when searching for movie names, you would be naturally more drawn to the story or writer. With Google prompts it's easy to lose yourself in a rabbit hole of information that you never meant to look into in the first place.

Measuring Success

With Knowledge Panel, what we ideally would want to measure is value added to the user and if we presented them with the answers they were looking for in the least time possible (in tandem with reduced effort - minimal clicks).

Measuring if the user did not click on any link beyond the first search would be an indicator that they found everything they were looking for in the panel itself. This could however prove to be false positive in some scenarios where maybe they didn't find what they were looking for at all and hence did not browse. Another false positive could be considering a further click as a bad thing, when maybe the user found the answer and was interested in learning more - however, in this case we could differentiate between clicks on the knowledge panel vs the main panel

If we truly value user satisfaction though, we need a way to measure if the snapshot provided user with the right information

  • Avoid Knowledge panels for convoluted and more sensitive public interest topics - because snapshots always dilute context and nuance to various degrees
  • Validate the sources used for panels - Wikipedia, for instance, with an 'anyone can edit' model, is not a reliable validated source of information
  • Transparency - citing sources and flagging non-validated sources
  • Feedback - strong guidelines to remove inaccuracies

Knowledge Graph concerns and inaccuracies4

Lack of Source Attribution

Google’s Knowledge Graph, an advanced database sourced largely from Wikipedia and constructed in part from user search patterns. According to a October 2015 analysis by the digital marketing firm Stone Temple Consulting, these knowledge panels, which are frequently unattributed, are one of the fastest growing types in Google’s arsenal.5

As of 2020, Google seems to have always attributed the souce. The transparency gives the power back to the user to decide for themselves if it is pulled from a trusted source and to dig deeper for nuances.4

Lack of Context

To Google, that’s proof of its semantic search technology; to Googlers, it’s a convenience that saves them a few clicks. But to skeptics, of whom there are a growing number, it’s a looming public literacy threat.

“It undermines people’s ability to verify information and, ultimately, to develop well-informed opinions,” said Dario Taraborelli, head of research at the Wikimedia Foundation and a social computing researcher who studies knowledge production online. “And that is something I think we really need to study and process as a society.” 

For Taraborelli, the primary issue with Google’s knowledge panels is that they aren’t terribly knowledgeable: They provide information but often leave out any context on where that information came from.

Being transparent by citing the source, coupled with allowing users to provide feedback and presenting relevant related details would help combat the lack of context to a large degree. Keeping in mind that with public interest topics no room for error should be allowed.

Bias and Inaccuracy

Mark Graham, a geographer at the Oxford Internet Institute, recently did just that: He and his colleague Heather Ford analyzed, in a paper published last month in the academic volume Code and the City, how the city of Jerusalem was represented both on Wikipedia and in Google knowledge panels. They found that while Wikipedia may explain the city’s contested geopolitical status in enormous depth — as of this writing, that portion runs to almost 1,500 words — the nuance was jettisoned completely when the article was deboned and ingested by Google.

As of 2020, Google has introduced a 'Feedback' link at the bottom of the panel for users to flag inaccuracies. Google then users the feedback to analyse the cause and to prevent inaccuracies int he future. They also delete inaccuracies that violate their policies, especially associated with public interest topics.4
