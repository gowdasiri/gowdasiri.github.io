Regulation

September 2023
EU · USA · Canada · Global

Governments around the world are increasingly setting rules that govern how data moves, how platforms compete, and how technology companies can operate. The EU has led — but the pressure is spreading.

Providing a paid, ad-free tier has become one of the most tangible examples of how companies are having to redesign products to comply with data privacy rules and other government policies — particularly in Europe. What was once optional product design is becoming regulatory compliance.

European Union

The EU's tech-focused legal framework

GDPR

General Data Protection Regulation

Enacted 2018. Landmark legislation protecting people's online data. Governs how companies collect, store, and use personal data.

DSA

Digital Services Act

Took effect 2023. Targets illegal content online and restricts personalized advertising to minors. Covers social networks, marketplaces, and content platforms.

DMA

Digital Markets Act

Taking effect 2024. Forces large "gatekeeper" platforms to change business practices to encourage competition. Apple expected to allow alternative app stores on EU devices.

GDPR — General Data Protection Regulation

In July 2023, the EU's highest court effectively barred Meta from combining data collected across its platforms — Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp — as well as from outside websites and apps, without explicit user consent. In January, Meta was fined €390 million by Irish regulators for forcing users to accept personalized ads as a condition of using Facebook.

"This shows that tech companies are complying with the E.U.'s digital regulations, suggesting that they remain beholden to governments and not the other way around."

— Anu Bradford, Columbia University Law Professor, author of Digital Empires

Europe is Meta's second most lucrative region after North America — EU advertising accounts for roughly 10% of the company's overall business, on total revenue of nearly $117 billion. The stakes of compliance are significant.

DSA & DMA — Digital Services Act & Digital Markets Act

The DSA and DMA form a single set of rules applying across the entire EU, with two goals: to create a safer digital space in which users' fundamental rights are protected, and to establish a level playing field that fosters competition and innovation.

Following the DSA, TikTok and Instagram users in Europe can now block personal data from being used to generate their feeds. Snapchat and Meta have stopped marketers from targeting 13–17 year-olds with personalized ads.

Why now? Some large platforms have emerged as gatekeepers in digital markets — private rule-makers with the power to act as bottlenecks between businesses and consumers. Their rules sometimes result in unfair conditions for businesses using their platforms and reduced choice for consumers. The DSA and DMA are designed to address exactly this.

The DMA takes effect in 2024, with wide-ranging consequences. Apple is expected to allow users in the EU to download apps from alternatives to the App Store on iPhones and iPads — for the first time.

Real-world impact

Meta barred from combining cross-platform user data without explicit consent (CJEU ruling, 2023)
Meta fined €390M for forcing ad consent as condition of Facebook access
TikTok & Instagram users can now opt out of algorithmic feed personalization
Snapchat & Meta prohibited from targeting 13–17 year-olds with personalized advertising
Apple expected to open iOS to third-party app stores in EU markets
Beyond Europe

Global regulation landscape

United States

Two significant moves at the state level. Utah passed a law that could curb the use of TikTok and Instagram by children and teenagers. California's Governor signed a sweeping Children's Online Safety Bill, requiring platforms to prioritize the safety of minors in their design decisions.

Canada

Canada passed the Online News Act in June 2023, joining a growing push by governments to force large social media companies to compensate news organizations. The law requires platforms and search engines to negotiate with news publishers to license their content. Both Meta and Google responded by blocking Canadian news from their platforms.

India & China

Both countries have enacted significant tech regulation, though through very different frameworks — India through data protection legislation and content moderation rules, China through sweeping state controls over data, algorithms, and platform behavior. These remain active and evolving areas.

"For two decades, tech platforms operated largely beyond the reach of traditional regulation — too new, too fast, too global for governments to keep up. That era is ending. The EU moved first and most aggressively, but the pattern is now global: governments are no longer asking platforms to self-regulate. They're legislating."
