Too Good To Go is a for-profit company aiming to be part of a global movement fighting food waste. Restaurants, cafes, and markets can list surprise bags containing end-of-day leftovers at a discounted price — roughly a third of the retail cost — generating additional revenue while ensuring as little food as possible is wasted. Founded in 2015 by Dutch women, the platform is now available in 17 countries.

The Model

How it works

While a restaurant or cafe may not know exactly what food will be left over at the end of each day, they know with certainty that something will be. Fresh food left overnight is stale by morning. The idea: pack whatever remains into a surprise bag, list it on the app at a third of the cost, and let customers pay and schedule pickup within a seller-defined window.

Packaging a surprise bag takes the same time — often less — than disposing of end-of-day leftovers. It brings in new customers, generates additional revenue, and requires no inventory calculation. Sellers simply add whatever is left.

The app receives a fixed cut on all purchases. Joining is free, and stores can begin listing bags from the day they sign up.

Seller Concerns

Pain Points — addressed

01

Adoption — is it more work?

The concern is whether participating takes more time than simply throwing food away. With the surprise bag model, managers do not need to calculate inventory of what remains — they add whatever is left to a bag. This takes as much or even less time than disposing of end-of-day leftovers. Onboarding is free and straightforward, and stores can list bags from day one.

02

Devaluing the brand

If you can get a bagel through TGTG at a fraction of the price, why would you ever pay full price? Two things protect against this: the surprise bag mechanic means you never know exactly what you'll get — if you need bread for dinner, you go to the bakery. And TGTG tends to bring in new customers who might not have visited otherwise and can convert to regulars. More broadly, sustainability adds brand value, not dilutes it.

Business Model

Why not a non-profit?

It shouldn't have to be mutually exclusive — a charity that helps the world, or a business that makes money. The premise of Too Good To Go is that social impact businesses can and should do both. To have a big impact, you need a strong business model to maximize it.

"To have a big impact, you need a strong business model to maximize the impact."

A significant part of this was raising capital through Bliss B Corp venture capital, whose north star metric was different from traditional VC. They were interested not only in financial KPIs, but in how the company operates — how it treats consumers, employees, and approaches diversity, equity, and inclusion. Building a mission-driven business that had to be sustainable and had to make money.

Social Impact

Are they undercutting charities?

The criticism is fair to raise: TGTG enables people who already have money to access discounted food, rather than redistributing it to those who are hungry or homeless — which is traditionally what charities do.

The concern

Surplus food sold at a discount goes to paying customers, not food banks. Doesn't this compete with redistribution efforts?

The reality

TGTG works with charities directly — food is first offered to food banks, and only the remainder is sold on the app. In many cases, restaurants had previously tried to donate to food banks, but it was logistically infeasible. TGTG bridges that gap.

In practice, surplus food on the app is frequently purchased by low-income families. And in many scenarios where no charity pickup was feasible, Too Good To Go ensures the food reaches someone rather than a bin.

"The surprise bag is a quietly elegant design decision — it removes the inventory problem for sellers, sets accurate expectations for buyers, and makes the whole model scale without friction. The best product thinking often works like that: solving an operational problem and a behavioral one at the same time."
