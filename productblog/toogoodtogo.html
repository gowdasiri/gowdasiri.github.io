40% of all food produced on the planet is wasted, responsible for 10% of green house gas emissions.

60% of food waste is through grocery stores, restaurants and other institutions, based on a research by FeedAmerica.

Too Good To Go is a for-profit company, aiming to be a part of a global movement fighting food waste. Restaurants, cafes and markets can list surprise bags containing end of day leftovers for a discounted price on the platform - as part of their sustainability mission to ensure as little food as possible is wasted and to generate additional revenue. Founded in 2015 by Dutch women, this platform is now available in 17 companies, as of 2023 and lists about 12k stores in the US.

Too Good To Go

While the restaurant, cafe or market is unaware of the food that will be leftover at the end of day, they are aware that they have food left every night. The leftover fresh food would be stale by the following day. The idea is to pack this food in surprise bags and sell it for a third of the cost on the app and avoid wastage end of day. The consumer pays for the order ahead time and picks up these bags within the window mentioned by the seller.

Brings in new customers, additional revenue and takes the same or even lesser time in packaging the food as it would disposing it.

Paint Point

  1. Adoption : Would it take more than than simply disposing off leftovers at the end of the day? At the end of the day, with surprise bag, the managers do not need to calculate inventory of what they have left, and instead simply add to bag anything that they have left over. This would take as much or even lesser time than getting rid of end of day leftovers. Joining the app is straightforward, free and stores can list bags from the day they join the app. The app then receives a fixed cut on all purchases.
  2. Devaluing the brand : If you can get a bagel with TGTG for a fraction of the cost, why would you pay full price? With a surprise bag, you are also not going to know what you're going to get. For instance, if you needed to buy bread for dinner, you would go to the bakery to buy bread, as opposed to purchase the surprise bag, since you don't know for sure if you would get bread. This would also bring in new customers through your doors, who could potentially convert to regular customers. And most importantly, sustainability is the future and add value to the brand as a whole.

Why not do it as a non profit?

It shouldn't have to be so mutually exclusive that you have a charity to help the world or a business to make money. The idea is that social impact businesses can do both and should do both. To have a big impact, you need a strong business model to maximize the impact.

Big part of this was also raising money with Bliss B Corp venture capital, because the North Star metric was different. They were not only interested in financial KPI, but also about the way they do things - deal with consumers, employees, diversity, equity, inclusion. Building a business with a mission, which had to be sustainable and had to make money.

Are they undercutting work of charities?

Enabling people who have money to access food, as opposed to charities where food would be redistributed to people are hungry or homeless.

They work with charities, to ensure that the food is first shared with the food bank and then the rest is sold on the app. In many cases, the food on the app is bought by low income families and in some other cases there are restaurants that have previously reached out to food banks to pick up the leftovers, but it was infeasible for them to do so. In a lot of different scenarios Too Good To Go bridges that gap in making the food accessible to many.
