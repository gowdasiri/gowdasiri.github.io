Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia, that anyone can edit.

With 61M articles in total as of late 2023 and 10% of those in English (English Wikipedia receives 48% of Wikipedia's cumulative traffic), Wikipedia, run by a non-profit organization, has consistently been one of the 10 most visited websites.

Brief History

In 1994, Ward Cunnigham created Wiki (wiki, the Hawaiin word for quick) - websites that allowed users to easily create and edit information on sites in real time.

In 2000, Jimmy Wales and Larry Sanger found Nupedia, a for-profit encyclopedic website, with peer reviewed articles, where the reviewers were expert contributors (preferably PhDs) in their fields. The venture faced growing pains due to lack of resources and an intimidating and cumbersome review process.

In 2001, Wikipedia (pedia comes from Hellenistic Greek, meaning 'education') was started as a "feeder" project for Nupedia - a discussion forum, where the public could suggest potential topics and effectively collaborate on content to submit to Nupedia. Its integral policy of "neutral point-of-view" was codified in its first few months. I was originally intended to be a business for profit with ad revenue. The "anyone can edit" model proved successful (Wales has maintained that communities inherently scale), resulting in Wikipedia to have published 20,000 articles as of 2003, as opposed to 24 articles published in Nupedia. At this point in time, Nupedia was taken down and it's text was incorporated into Wikipedia. Sander disagreeing with the project, left at this point in time (more on this below). The English Wikipedia passed the mark of two million articles on September 9, 2007, making it the largest encyclopedia ever assembled.

Product Vision

Wales has stated the vision has been "democratization of knowledge" and to “compile the sum of all human knowledge.”

Few key decisions made at a nascent stage dictated both the quality of the end product and the community that it attracted and sustained.

  • Setting an editorial policy : Discussions on whether essay, commentary, jokes in articles should be allowed on articles with the community lead to the adoption of neutral point of view as a core belief.
  • Controls for vandalism
  • Validating quality of source
  • Community guidelines : When should a user be banned? Ethos of the community has been that with discussions it is nothing personal, and the debate is focused on how the article can be improved as opposed to debating the topic in itself.

Non profit organization - Decision driven by both the community and founders whose thought was to have the website remain a cultural contribution and not have it be driven by funds. Wales has since defended the decision stating it would've been very hard to stop an organization from following the money - caring a lot more about pages that drove ad revenue by higher page views/traffic. Wikipedia has been able to raise funds through successful fundraisers, being run on a shoestring budget, it has been able to sustain itself for the past couple of decades with some reserve revenue.

Target Audience

From a contributor's perspective, Wikipedia does not attract experts. In fact it discourages experts from contributing as the changes may be replaced.

Target Experience

Wikipedia aims to provide free access to the sum of all human knowledge.

However, in Wales' own words on the How I Built This podcast, "Wikipedia is quite good, it is the place to go to get the questions, not the answers. A way to get oriented into the context of the topic. Go to the Talk page of the article and see what the Wikipedians are struggling with, it presents interesting questions. If you want to use Wikipedia as the starting point, you can go to the footnotes and go to the source and that's what you should do."

Pain points

Cumbersome collaboration and intimidating review process

Using Wiki alleviated the cumbersome collaboration process editors previously faced on Nupedia. The anyone can edit model further ensured that this could scale, but attracting users who may have previously been intimidated by the review and submission process, and ensuring that the model did not depend on a few select contributors who were experts in the field.

Data quality concerns - Vandalism, Hoaxes, and manipulation

As is typical with Wikipedians, a response emerged from a mixture of cordial discussions, tedious arguments, and online wrestling matches—but it was sophisticated. The project’s most active volunteers introduced a raft of new editing tools and bureaucratic procedures intended to combat the bad edits. They created software that allowed fellow editors to quickly survey recent changes and reject them or admonish their authors with a single mouse click. They set loose automated “bots” that could reverse any incorrectly formatted changes or those that were likely to be vandalism and dispatch warning messages to the offending editors.

A frequently vandalized article can be "semi-protected" or "extended confirmed protected", meaning that only "autoconfirmed" or "extended confirmed" editors can modify it.[83]A particularly contentious article may be locked so that only administrators can make changes.[84]

Since 2007, when the new controls began to bite, the likelihood of a new participant’s edit being immediately deleted has steadily climbed. Over the same period, the proportion of those deletions made by automated tools rather than humans grew. Unsurprisingly, the data also indicate that well-intentioned newcomers are far less likely to still be editing Wikipedia two months after their first try.

Skewed coverage - biased towards technical, Western, and male-dominated subject matter

It has been criticized for exhibiting systemic bias, particularly gender bias against women and geographical bias against the Global South.

The main source of those problems is not mysterious. The loose collective running the site today, estimated to be 90 percent male, operates a crushing bureaucracy with an often abrasive atmosphere that deters newcomers who might increase participation in Wikipedia and broaden its coverage. “The biggest issue is editor diversity,” says Wales. He hopes to “grow the number of editors in topics that need work.”1

Because Wikipedia has failed to replenish its supply of editors, its skew toward technical, Western, and male-dominated subject matter has persisted. In 2011, researchers from the University of Minnesota and three other schools showed that articles worked on mostly by female editors—which presumably were more likely to be of interest to women—were significantly shorter than those worked on mostly by male editors or by men and women equally. Another 2011 study, from the University of Oxford, found that 84 percent of entries tagged with a location were about Europe or North America. Antarctica had more entries than any nation in Africa or South America.

While this could be combated by encouraging contributions and growing active diverse editors, newcomers to Wikipedia making their first, tentative edits—and the inevitable mistakes—have become less likely to stick around. Being steamrollered by the newly efficient, impersonal editing machine was no fun. The number of active editors on the English-language Wikipedia peaked in 2007 at more than 51,000 and has been declining ever since as the supply of new ones got choked off. This past summer only 31,000 people could be considered active editors.

Decline in page views since 2014

On January 20, 2014, Subodh Varma reporting for The Economic Times indicated that not only had Wikipedia's growth stalled, it "had lost nearly ten percent of its page views last year. There was a decline of about two billion between December 2012 and December 2013. Its most popular versions are leading the slide: page-views of the English Wikipedia declined by twelve percent, those of German version slid by 17 percent and the Japanese version lost nine percent."[71] Varma added, "While Wikipedia's managers think that this could be due to errors in counting, other experts feel that Google's Knowledge Graphs project launched last year may be gobbling up Wikipedia users."[71] When contacted on this matter, Clay Shirky, associate professor at New York University and fellow at Harvard's Berkman Klein Center for Internet & Society said that he suspected much of the page-view decline was due to Knowledge Graphs, stating, "If you can get your question answered from the search page, you don't need to click [any further]."

Tradeoffs

If you, like me have wondered, whether an encyclopedia as large as this one could've been achieved by reviewed contributors and subject matter experts, there is a website that attempted the feat. Larry Sanger, one of the founders of Wikipedia, launched a free online encyclopedia called Citizendium, with the vision, "The world needs a more credible free encyclopedia". It planned to achieve this by requiring virtually all contributors to use their real names, by strictly moderating the project for unprofessional behavior, by providing "gentle expert oversight" of everyday contributors, and through "approved articles" which have undergone a form of peer-review by topic experts with credentials.

It however failed to scale, since it was unable to grow and sustain contributors.

Experts are required to verify their qualifications openly, for transparency and publicly accepted authority.[25] This contrasts with the open and largely anonymous nature of Wikipedia,[26] where subject specialists have neither any verifiable special knowledge of their subject nor agreed special status. Sanger stated that editors would not have pre-approval rights over edits by ordinary authors, though editors would have somewhat undefined authority over articles that fall within their specific area of expertise.

Unlike Wikipedia, Citizendium does not allow anonymous editing. Participants must register under their real names with a working email address. Sanger decided that Citizendium administrators would be called "constables", and need a bachelor's degree to qualify. He also instituted a minimum "maturity" requirement—25 years of age—for constables.[25] The "head" constable is the Chief Constable (D. Matt Innis), and the head editor is the Managing Editor.[27]

Citizendium articles are subject to an "approval" process after they have achieved reasonable quality. An "editor" can determine when an article is ready to be approved.[25]
