Origin

Brief History

In 1994, Ward Cunningham created Wiki — wiki, the Hawaiian word for "quick" — websites that allowed users to easily create and edit information in real time.

In 2000, Jimmy Wales and Larry Sanger founded Nupedia, a for-profit encyclopedic website with peer-reviewed articles, where reviewers were expert contributors (preferably PhDs) in their fields. The venture faced growing pains due to lack of resources and an intimidating, cumbersome review process.

In 2001, Wikipedia (pedia from Hellenistic Greek, meaning "education") was started as a "feeder" project for Nupedia — a discussion forum where the public could suggest topics and collaborate on content. Its core policy of neutral point-of-view was codified in its first few months.

The "anyone can edit" model proved transformative. Wales maintained that communities inherently scale — and the data bore this out: Wikipedia published 20,000 articles by 2003, compared to just 24 published in Nupedia over the same period. Nupedia was taken down, its text incorporated into Wikipedia. The English Wikipedia passed two million articles on September 9, 2007, making it the largest encyclopedia ever assembled.

Vision

Product Vision

Wales has stated the vision as "democratization of knowledge" and to "compile the sum of all human knowledge." A few key decisions made at the nascent stage dictated both the quality of the end product and the community it attracted.

  • Editorial policy — Discussions on whether essays, commentary, and jokes should be allowed led to the adoption of neutral point of view as a core belief.
  • Controls for vandalism — A combination of community tools, automated bots, and tiered protection levels.
  • Source validation — Verifiability over truth: claims must be attributable to reliable, published sources.
  • Community guidelines — When should a user be banned? The ethos: debates are about improving the article, not the topic itself. Nothing personal.

Non-profit organization — A decision driven by both the community and the founders, whose view was that the site should remain a cultural contribution, not be driven by funds. Wales has defended it: it would've been very hard to stop an organization from following the money — caring more about pages that drove ad revenue through higher page views. Wikipedia has sustained itself for two decades through fundraisers and a shoestring budget.

Community

Users

Contributors

~75,000 monthly contributors making ~5 edits each. A core community of 3,000–5,000 making 100+ edits.

Admins

A small group elected by the community, wielding special powers such as deleting articles or temporarily banning editors. Currently 635 active admins on the English Wikipedia.

Target Audience

From a contributor's perspective, Wikipedia does not attract experts — in fact, it actively discourages them, since any expert's edits can be replaced by anyone. The model depends on a broad, self-correcting community rather than credentialed authority.

Target Experience

Wikipedia aims to provide free access to the sum of all human knowledge. But in Wales' own words on How I Built This:

"Wikipedia is quite good — it is the place to go to get the questions, not the answers. A way to get oriented into the context of a topic. If you want to use it as a starting point, go to the footnotes and go to the source. That's what you should do."

Challenges

Pain Points

Cumbersome collaboration & intimidating review

Using Wiki alleviated the cumbersome collaboration process editors faced on Nupedia. The "anyone can edit" model ensured it could scale — attracting users previously intimidated by the review and submission process, and ensuring the model didn't depend on a few select experts.

Data quality — Vandalism, hoaxes, and manipulation

The community's response was sophisticated. Active volunteers introduced new editing tools and bureaucratic procedures. They created software allowing editors to quickly survey recent changes and reject them with a single click. They deployed automated bots that could reverse incorrectly formatted changes or likely vandalism and dispatch warnings to offending editors.

A frequently vandalized article can be "semi-protected," meaning only autoconfirmed editors can modify it. A particularly contentious article may be locked so only administrators can make changes.

Since 2007, when new controls began to bite, the likelihood of a new participant's edit being immediately deleted has steadily climbed — and well-intentioned newcomers are far less likely to still be editing Wikipedia two months after their first try.

Skewed coverage — biased toward technical, Western, and male-dominated subject matter

The loose collective running the site today is estimated to be 90 percent male, operating a crushing bureaucracy with an often abrasive atmosphere that deters newcomers. "The biggest issue is editor diversity," Wales has said.

A 2011 study from the University of Minnesota showed that articles worked on mostly by female editors were significantly shorter than those worked on mostly by male editors. Another 2011 study from Oxford found that 84 percent of entries tagged with a location were about Europe or North America. Antarctica had more entries than any nation in Africa or South America.

The number of active editors on English Wikipedia peaked in 2007 at more than 51,000 and has been declining ever since — this past summer only 31,000 people could be considered active editors.

Trend

Decline in Page Views Since 2014

On January 20, 2014, The Economic Times reported that Wikipedia had lost nearly ten percent of its page views the prior year — a decline of about two billion between December 2012 and December 2013. The English Wikipedia declined by 12%, German by 17%, Japanese by 9%.

The likely culprit: Google's Knowledge Graph, launched the prior year. Clay Shirky, associate professor at NYU and fellow at Harvard's Berkman Klein Center, put it plainly:

"If you can get your question answered from the search page, you don't need to click any further."

Comparison

Tradeoffs — Wikipedia vs. Citizendium

If you've wondered whether an encyclopedia this large could've been achieved by reviewed contributors and subject matter experts, there's a website that attempted the feat. Larry Sanger, one of Wikipedia's co-founders, launched Citizendium with the vision: "The world needs a more credible free encyclopedia."

It failed to scale — unable to grow and sustain contributors.

Citizendium

Real names required with working email
Expert contributors must verify qualifications openly
Gentle expert oversight of everyday contributors
Admins ("constables") require a bachelor's degree, age 25+
Articles subject to a formal approval process
Failed to sustain contributor growth

The lesson is stark: credibility and scale are in fundamental tension. Wikipedia chose scale — and got both reach and the biases that come with it. Citizendium chose credibility — and got neither.

"Wales built something genuinely unprecedented — a living document of human knowledge, written by no one and everyone, sustained by community rather than capital. Its flaws are inseparable from its scale. The better question isn't whether it's perfect, but whether anything else could have worked."
