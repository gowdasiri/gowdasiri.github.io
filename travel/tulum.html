Planning

Best time to visit

The best time to visit Tulum depends on what you prioritize — weather, avoiding crowds, or budget. Here's a breakdown:

High Season

December — April

Best weather — warm, low humidity, minimal rain. Peak crowds and prices, especially around Christmas, New Year, and Easter. Various festivals add vibrancy.

Shoulder

May — June · November

Warm and enjoyable with occasional showers. Fewer tourists, lower rates. Beaches and cenotes are less crowded — a more peaceful visit overall.

Low Season

July — October

Rainy season. Hurricane risk August–October. Highest humidity. Fewest crowds and significantly cheaper rates — good for budget travelers. Some businesses may have limited hours.

For the best balance of weather, fewer crowds, and reasonable prices — November and early December are the sweet spot. Just before high season begins, with pleasant weather and a quieter atmosphere.

Hurricanes are most likely between August and October. Worth factoring in when planning.

Day by day

6-Day Itinerary

Day 01

Arrival & Beach Relaxation

Playa Paraiso Las Palmas Playa Ruinas La Eufemia Beach Club

Morning: Arrive in Tulum — the airport is a 30–40 minute drive from the downtown beach area. Check in and settle.

Afternoon: Head to Playa Paraiso, one of Tulum's most stunning beaches. Relax, swim, and ease into Caribbean mode. For a more laid-back vibe, bring your own towel and drinks to any of the true public-access beaches — Las Palmas, Playa Paraiso, or Playa Ruinas. All equally beautiful, no minimum spend required.

Evening: Dinner at a beachfront restaurant like La Eufemia Beach Club for delicious food with a view.

Day 02

Tulum Ruins & Cenotes

Tulum Ruins Sac Actun Tulum Pueblo Bonita / Burrito Amor

Morning: Visit the Tulum Ruins early — beat the crowds and the heat. The views of the ancient Mayan port city from the cliff above the sea are worth every bit of the early alarm.

Afternoon: Cool off at Sac Actun, about a 30-minute drive from the ruins. You have to book a guided tour, which involves a combination of walking and snorkeling through an underground connected cave system. I couldn't recommend it highly enough — the tour lasts about 90 minutes and all gear (wetsuit, snorkel equipment) is included.

Other cenote recommendations: Oxman, Gran Cenote, Dos Ojos, Car Wash (crystal-clear waters), Casa Cenote, Zacil-Ha, Calavera, Casa Tortuga. Whichever you choose, plan to pay an entrance fee (minimum $8 USD) plus an additional fee for cameras or drones.

Evening: Explore Tulum Pueblo and dinner at Bonita's or Burrito Amor for tasty, casual dining.

Day 03

Day Trip — Chichén Itzá, Valladolid & Cenote Ik Kil

Chichén Itzá Cenote Ik Kil Hacienda Chichen Valladolid Palma Central

Morning: Depart early — cannot stress this enough. Sharp 7am at the latest. Chichén Itzá is a 2–2.5 hour drive from Tulum. Explore El Castillo, the Temple of the Warriors, and the Great Ball Court. Tour guides are available at and near the gate — make sure yours speaks your language fluently before hiring them.

Afternoon: Cool off at a cenote near Chichén Itzá. Ik Kil is less than 10 minutes away and absolutely stunning. Lorenzo Oxman and Suytun are also worth considering.

Lunch: Hacienda Chichen — a gorgeous Spanish mansion converted to a hotel, located a few minutes from Chichén Itzá.

Late Afternoon: Stop in Valladolid — a charming, colorful colonial town many skip in favor of heading straight back to Tulum. Worth it for the church, the park, the shops, and churros.

Evening: Return to Tulum. Dinner at Palma Central.

Day 04

Sian Ka'an Biosphere Reserve

Sian Ka'an Reserve Punta Allen Mateo's

Morning: Take a guided tour of the Sian Ka'an Biosphere Reserve — a UNESCO World Heritage Site with opportunities to spot dolphins, turtles, and manatees among diverse wildlife and mangroves.

Afternoon: Continue the tour with bird watching and boat rides through the lagoons. The tour takes you near the world's second largest coral reef for snorkeling. Stop for lunch in Punta Allen, a small village on the eastern tip of Mexico with fresh, delicious seafood.

Evening: Back to Tulum. Dinner at Mateo's, known for its fish tacos.

Day 05

Day Trip — Playa del Carmen & Akumal

Cenote Azul Akumal — Sea Turtles La Quinta Avenida Mezzanine

Morning: Breakfast in Tulum, then head to Cenote Azul — beautiful shades of blue, 20 minutes from Playa del Carmen. Great for swimming and snorkeling. Cenote Chaak Aktun is another option, with cave tours available.

Afternoon: Akumal Beach — try your luck swimming with sea turtles. Rent life vests and stay near areas with seagrass, which draws the turtles close to shore for feeding and nesting.

Then drive to Playa del Carmen (about an hour from Tulum) and explore La Quinta Avenida — a pedestrian street full of shops, restaurants, and cafes. Get lunch at a restaurant with a 2nd-floor view of La Quinta; the people-watching is a blast. (Full Playa del Carmen guide here.)

Evening: Back to Tulum for dinner at Mezzanine.

Day 06

Relaxation & Departure

Mezzanine Departure

Morning: Spend your last morning at Mezzanine — enjoy the facilities, the beach, and a leisurely breakfast or brunch before heading to the airport.

Logistics

Tips

01

Rent a car

Renting a car is the most convenient way to get around Tulum and do day trips. We paid ~$200 USD for a 6-day SUV rental from Hertz at the airport (full coverage included). Expect to wait up to an hour for pickup, and another 30 minutes at drop-off. Budget an additional $50–70 for diesel. Worth noting: some gas stations only accept cash, and attendants pump your gas and wipe your windshield. Read Mexico's road rules, and remember — Tope means speed bump.

02

Carry cash in pesos

Quite a few places prefer cash, and tipping is much easier in pesos. Have some on hand at all times — especially for street food, cenote entries, and gas stations.

03

Get travel insurance

We used IMG (~$25) and Berkshire Hathaway (~$7) for a 6-day trip. WorldNomads is another great option for adventurous travelers. Full comparison here.

04

Book popular tours & restaurants in advance

Sac Actun, Sian Ka'an, and Chichén Itzá tours all benefit from pre-booking. Same goes for popular restaurants — especially those along the beach strip.

Geology

How is a cenote formed?

Cenotes form through the gradual dissolution of limestone bedrock over thousands of years — a geological process unique to places like the Yucatán Peninsula:

01

Limestone formation. The bedrock in the region is composed of limestone — a porous rock formed from the accumulation of marine organisms like corals and shellfish over millions of years.

02

Underground caves develop. Rainwater picks up carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and soil, creating weak carbonic acid. As this percolates through cracks in the limestone, it dissolves the rock, enlarging fractures and forming underground channels and cave systems.

03

Roof collapse. Over time, the ceilings of these underground caves become unstable from erosion. Eventually they collapse, exposing the underground water to the surface — forming a cenote.

04

What you get. Cenotes vary widely in size and shape — from small circular openings to vast sinkholes with vertical walls. They can be filled with freshwater, saltwater, or a mix, depending on location and connection to underground aquifers.

05

Why they matter. Cenotes are essential freshwater sources in the Yucatán, where surface water is scarce. They're also windows into the underground water systems and home to unique biodiversity found nowhere else on earth.

Beach clubs

Beach clubs

Coco Tulum

A great budget-friendly option — minimum consumption of only $25, which is very low for Tulum beach. The quality is excellent: three fish tacos come with spicy pineapple salsa, tomato salsa, and guacamole. Sandwiches (including deep-fried prawns) come with a generous side of potatoes — they don't skimp on anything.

cocotulum.com

Papaya Playa Project

Probably the hippest hotel and beach club in Tulum, and you'll pay for the honor. During low season, minimum consumption is typically $25–$65 USD — and you'll spend it quickly. The beach club menu includes ceviches, tacos, and tostadas; portions are generous, and one main dish is usually enough.

The fish ceviche with onions and avocado is fresh and excellent. The tuna tostada is loaded with avocado mousse — definitely bring a fork.

papayaplayaproject.com
